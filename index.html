Why It Matters

The lake is more than a landmark. It's a lifeline.

Stretching across the northern Utah landscape, the Great Salt Lake is the largest saltwater lake in the Western Hemisphere. It supports migratory birds, fuels the local economy, and moderates the regional climate. But decades of water diversion, drought, and population growth have pushed it to a historic low. We're here to document that crisis and demand change.

The Great Salt Lake: A Feedback Loop of Environmental Degradation

By Taplin Everette  ·  6 min read

← Back to Blog

As the Great Salt Lake dries, it reaches a critical juncture. Sustainability models predict that it will continue to dry as long as change is not made to save what little is left of the Lake. As record low water levels continue to drop, important environmental problems come to light.

Toxic Dust

The Great Salt Lake has long been burdened by an onslaught of Salt Lake City pollution. This often happens in the form of sediment pollution from urban runoff, thermal pollution from industrial practices, etc. Every industrial action gives rise to the amounts of heavy metals within the Great Salt Lake. Because the lake is a terminal lake - meaning it has no output - heavy metals settle within its bed. As the lake dries, however, these metals are released into the air, not only harming Salt Lake City residents’ respiratory and cardiovascular health but creating a loop of environmental degradation. As this meatal-infused dust is put into the air, it acts to warm Salt Lake City’s climate by lowering the reflectivity of snow and ice, lowering the amount of water that actually reaches the Great Salt Lake, expediting its drying.

The Brine Shrimp Food Web

The Great Salt Lake has long acted as a stopping point along migratory bird routes. A large reason this takes place is because of the prevalence of brine shrimp within the lake. Unfortunately, these shrimp have faced problems with the salinity of the lake. As the lake dries, the water evaporated leaves behind all the salt that was once dissolved in it, greatly increasing the salt content within the remaining water within the lake. This poses a massive threat to the brine shrimp populations within the lake as brine shrimp have a salinity tipping point of around 18%, greatly disturbing the millions of migratory bird populations that rely on the abundance of brine shrimp within the lake. Without change, this massively important food web could experience a colossal trophic cascade.

The 2034 Charter

To address the ever-growing threat of the Great Salt Lake’s drying, Utah environmental leaders have put a series of regulatory actions in place. The plan is to restore the lake’s elevation to a sustainable level before the 2034 Winter Olympics take place in Salt Lake City. This includes implementation of split-season leasing to encourage sustainable water use, municipal construction changes to make Salt Lake City buildings more environmentally friendly, and management to evaluate the salinity of the lake. The charter aims to restore the lake through both ecocentric and technocentric methods, ensuring the lake, and the people of Salt Lake Valley last as long as possible.

Our Mission

About This Project

A student-led effort to document the drying of the Great Salt Lake and inspire action through education and storytelling.

Why We Care

The Great Salt Lake is at the heart of Utah's identity and ecology. For thousands of years it has sustained migratory birds, supported indigenous communities, and shaped the climate of the Wasatch Front. Today, it faces an existential threat, and most people don't even know it.

This blog was created as a school project to raise awareness about a crisis happening in our own backyard. The lake has lost more than 70% of its historic water volume, and the exposed lakebed, called a playa, releases toxic dust that blankets communities across northern Utah.

What We're Doing

Through research, writing, and reporting, we aim to make the science accessible to everyone. We cover topics ranging from the lake's ecology and the wildlife that depends on it, to the political and agricultural decisions that have diverted its water supply, to the grassroots movements fighting to save it.

We believe that telling this story clearly and compellingly is one of the most important things we can do. If more people understand the stakes, more people will demand change. Change is still possible.

Get Involved

You can help by sharing these articles, supporting water conservation organizations in Utah, and contacting your elected representatives. We'd love to hear from you.
